*Tool: a handheld device that aids in accomplishing a task*

Above is just one of many ways I think you could define the word "tool". The Merriam-Webster associates the word with something hand-held and physical; and most of us would agree in that its probably the first thing we'd think of when it is brought up. A wrench and a screwdriver were the tools your grandpa needed to adjust the sporadic idle of his 1970s carbureated car. You could also say that a kitchen knife and a peeler were the tools your chef used to make your favorite dish at the Cheesecake Factory, or a pencil and notebook are the tools required by a student to learn the material in their geometry class. 

The last two examples may not very traditional conceptualizations of tools, but you can see where I'm going with this. 

## What makes a tool?
Great question! I'm glad you asked. Although we technically already defined what a tool is in the very first statement, I believe there is a much broader definition of a tool that does not have to be constrained to the physical realm. In my eyes, a tool is any thing or any concept that allows a person (or a group of people) to accomplish a certain task or achieve a certain goal in a manner that reduces the actual (or percieved) effort for user. 

This may not be a very revolutionary definition of the word, so lets give some more concrete examples: 

<ol>
  <li>Currency is a tool that helps individuals in an economy exchange goods and services of value</li>
  <li>Drawers are tools that help keep documents and folders organized and safely stored</li>
  <li>The internet is a tool for exchanging information across the globe with ease and convenience</li>
</ol>

There are also less concrete examples that, if you could look at it sideways, could also be considered tools:

<ol>
  <li>Math and science are a tools for learning about the natural world</li>
  <li>Idioms such as "beating a dead horse" are tools for representing certain social circumstances like having to repeating something over and over again, while also conveying the sense of annoyance towards that circumstances.</li>
  <li>Bootstrap 5 is a tool that allows web developers to integrate dynamic web design with HTML and CSS with a higher degree of efficiency</li>
</ol>

## In the context of programming

That last example seemed oddly specific, but its true. And like any useful tool there will be a learning curve to it, as I found out very recently. 

Bootstrap is a User Interface (UI) Framework that helps developers build websites. I can describe it as similar to how the .underscore library provides extra built-in functionality within Javascript to help software engineers spend less time fiddling around with syntax and more time designing the program for their intended use. If you play video games, I can also describe it as similar to a DLC pack, where the extra content is not required for the game but may allow for your experience to be much more fulfilling and enjoyable. Okay, maybe not the greatest analogy, but I think you understand.

Learning the basic concept of how Bootstrap works and how it can help build a website is fairly intuitive with its grid and container formatting scheme, but like anything difficult, actually doing it is the challenging part at first. There is an abundance of classes that each element of the page can inherit to modify their attributes and behaviors as different conditions within the web page are met, from scrolling to re-sizing, hovering, and clicking. There is also a lack of the sense of "cause and effect" where changing something small in your code may not always reflect on the web page since there are a lot of invisible elements such as containers, rows, and margin/padding.

## So, is Bootstrap not worth learning then?

Well as you can say with a lot of things in life, if its hard then there's probably a reason to believe that its worth getting/achieving in some regard. Nobody would feel very accomplished if they finally got the promotion at work only to find out that so did everybody else in their team. Similarly, many tools in life require some degree of competence to effectly handle in order to reap the most benefits from it, and its no different with Bootstrap. Just from watching the example websites that other developers have made using this framework, I can tell that there is a degree of fluidity that one can achieve when mastering Bootstrap that only comes with days, or perhaps weeks, of continuous repetition and practice with the inner-workings of the structure. When thinking in the long-term, this is definitely a handy tool to keep in your toolbox. Just make sure to brush the dust off of it every once in a while!
